Word-watching commentary

The second exploration of the Distant Reading Project was a class collaboration effort. Each student still visualized five issues of their respective digital rhetoric journals with added constraints: body-text only (no author’s biographies or bibliographies) and three stop words (digital, rhetoric, and rhetorical). These constraints allowed clearer of visualization of words associated beyond the often-times typical association with the terms digital and rhetoric.

These “new” Word Clouds did not change as drastically as expect. While this change may be due to the limitations of stop word choice, the words most frequented in each issue of XChanges still follows an associated pattern with other words associated with digital rhetoric.

  • Issue 11.2: Driscoll, Ethos, James, Audience, Science
  • Issue 12.1: Users, Writers, Technology, Design, Social
  • Issue 12.2/13.1: Blog, Students, Meat, Processed, Red
  • Issue 13.2: Feedback, Students, Participants, Writing, Games
  • Issue 14.1: Archiving, Participants, Websites, Communication, Information

What questions, patterns, or insights did your word clouds provoke?

Briefly glancing through the most frequented words of each XChanges issue there is no inherent association with either digital or rhetoric. Their association with either term may be solely based on the context in which they’re published, in this case the journal XChanges. This idea led me to consider why any words are associated with a discipline or genre. As previously stated in my first project, XChanges publishes twice annually with one issue dedicated to undergraduate work with the other dedicated to graduate and professional research. The use of stop words (digital, rhetoric, and rhetorical) do not limited the association of words because the producer and consumer of the product have consciously or subconsciously already associated the words in correlation with digital rhetoric.

The addition of stop words has disruption the initial patterns found in my first project. However, this disruption has led me to other questions regarding the use of word clouds and digital rhetoric. If articles are to be published in journals that advertise as digital rhetoric journals, are key terms required or just encouraged? If the words are required, why? We have discussed in class how the definition of rhetoric, and even more so digital rhetoric, is in constant flux. Rhetoricians struggle with encompassing what the discipline is. As students, if our teacher/professors/experts-of-the-field are unsure of this definition, then the word clouds dedication to student work can provide insight to the future direction of the field.

How has this distant reading helped you make sense of "digital rhetoric"?

My first project attempt explored the reliability of word clouds, especially with such a small sample of XChanges and with my classmates’ word clouds. XChanges Issue 12.2/13.1’s most frequented words are drastically different from the others. Why? When looking at the word clouds, even the least frequently used words are similar, what does this suggested about this issue? Word Clouds visualize the most frequently used words, not the most useful. These Word Clouds provide a preview of the article content, but alone, the words are not useful: Meat, Processed, Red. A word’s usefulness is determined by its context and my Word Clouds are no different. The associations between words assist in determining how journals correlate with one another as they work under the genre digital rhetoric.

This project highlighted the flexibility of digital rhetoric as a term and as a genre. Each of my Word Clouds demonstrates how many articles and research interests fall under this genre, because of their preadmitted association with the key terms digital and rhetoric. I do not think this project has helped me make sense of digital rhetoric because of the complexities of language. Word associations are fascinating to me because of the images they conjure either because I have been socialized to their meanings (as per a classmate’s example, mist and castles are associated with gothic texts) or experience. From these associations, and my discussions amongst my classmates, words cannot act alone. By extension, our old and new Word Clouds do not make meaning alone. They create meaning based on the interactions of myself and my selected audience members, but we can only communicate through a predetermined common ground. This common ground relies on the word associations of digital and rhetoric. This project has allowed me to further explore how impactful word associations are, and not just the sheer frequencies.
